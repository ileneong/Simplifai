How to use this guide

A good prompt does not replace your judgement. It gets you to a first draft in minutes, so you are editing, not staring at a blank page.

  1. Copy the prompt. Fill in the brackets. Anything in [square brackets] is your bit. The more real detail you give, the better the draft.
  2. Treat every output as a first draft. You are the expert. The prompt gets you started; you make the final call on what goes out.
  3. Keep your voice. Where a prompt asks for "my voice," paste two or three things you have actually written so the AI can match how you sound.
  4. Make them yours. Change the wording, tighten the constraints, combine two prompts. These are starting points, not rules.
One rule above all

Never paste a customer's name or personal details into an AI tool. Strip the personal bits first and keep it about the task. Credible beats fast, every time.

01
Workflow
Customer support
The same questions, answered well, without you retyping them.
1
Draft a reply to a common question
"A customer asked: [paste their message]. Here is how I usually handle this: [paste a past reply or the key facts]. Write a clear, friendly reply in my voice that answers them fully and ends with a helpful next step. Under 120 words, no jargon."
2
Calm down a frustrated message
"A customer sent this, and they are upset: [paste message, names removed]. Write a calm, fair reply that acknowledges how they feel, takes responsibility where it is fair to, and offers a concrete way forward. Warm, not defensive, no over apologising."
3
Explain a policy without sounding cold
"I need to explain this policy to a customer: [paste the policy]. Rewrite it as a short, warm message that makes the reason clear and does not sound like fine print. 3 to 4 sentences, my voice."
4
Write a holding reply
"A customer needs an answer I cannot give yet because [reason]. Write a short holding reply that acknowledges their message, sets a realistic expectation for when I will get back to them, and reassures them it is in hand. 3 sentences."
5
Turn one good answer into a template
"Here is a reply I wrote that worked well: [paste it]. Turn it into a reusable template with [brackets] where the details change, so I can adapt it in seconds next time. Keep the tone exactly as it is."
6
Simplify a complex explanation
"I need to explain [complex thing] to a customer who is not an expert. Here is the detail: [paste it]. Rewrite it in plain language a busy person can understand on the first read. Short sentences, no jargon, one clear takeaway."
7
Say no, kindly
"A customer asked for [request] and I need to decline because [reason]. Write a reply that says no clearly but warmly, explains briefly, and offers an alternative if there is one. Respectful, not apologetic to the point of sounding unsure."
8
Apologise and fix a mistake
"We got something wrong: [what happened]. Write a short message that owns the mistake plainly, says what we are doing to fix it, and does not make excuses. Sincere, specific, my voice."
9
Build an FAQ from your inbox
"Here are questions customers ask me over and over: [list 8 to 12]. Draft a clear, on brand answer for each, 2 to 3 sentences. Flag any answer I should double check against my actual terms before publishing."
10
Write an after-hours auto-reply
"Write an out-of-office / after-hours auto-reply for my [business type] in my voice. It should set expectations for when I will respond, point them to [where to find quick info], and still feel human, not robotic. Under 60 words."

Workflow
Lead generation
Enquiries captured and moved forward, not lost in the inbox.
1
Qualify an enquiry
"A new lead sent this: [paste enquiry]. Write a friendly reply that answers what I can, and asks the 2 or 3 questions I need to know whether we are a fit and to give them a real answer. Do not interrogate, keep it warm."
2
Reply to "how much do you charge?"
"Someone asked what I charge for [service]. Here is how my pricing works: [paste the basics]. Write a reply that gives them a genuine sense of cost, explains what shapes the price, and invites them to the next step. No dodging the question, no hard sell."
3
Move a vague enquiry to a booked call
"A lead is interested but vague: [paste message]. Write a reply that shows I understood them, offers one clear next step (a short call or a booking), and makes it easy to say yes. 4 sentences."
4
Write a short intro about what you do
"Write me three versions of a short intro I can send a new lead who asks what I do. I am a [what you do] for [who you serve], and what makes me different is [your edge]. Plain, confident, no buzzwords. One sentence, two sentence, and short paragraph versions."
5
First nudge to a quiet lead
"A lead went quiet after showing interest in [service]. Write a light, no pressure follow up that adds a little value or reassurance rather than just chasing. 3 sentences, easy to reply to."
6
Sketch a lead magnet
"I want a simple free resource to attract [who you serve]. Give me 5 lead magnet ideas that solve a real, specific problem for them, and for the strongest one, outline what it would include. Practical, not fluffy."
7
Keep a waitlist warm
"I am fully booked and have a waitlist for [service]. Write a message for people on it that is honest about the wait, keeps them feeling valued, and gives them one thing to do in the meantime. Warm, not apologetic."
8
Sort a batch of enquiries
"Here are recent enquiries: [paste them, names removed]. Sort them into hot, warm, and cold based on how ready they seem to buy, and for each give me the one next action I should take. Short and practical."
9
Ask a happy customer for a referral
"I want to ask a happy customer to refer people like them. They came to me for [what you did]. Write a warm, specific referral ask that makes it easy and does not feel grabby. 3 to 4 sentences."
10
Sharpen your one-liner
"Help me write the one line that goes in my bio and introductions. I help [who] do [what] so they can [outcome]. Give me 5 versions, each under 15 words, plain and specific, no clichés like 'take your business to the next level.'"

Workflow
Feedback and reviews
The polite ask that goes out without you remembering, and what to do with the answers.
1
Ask for a review at the right moment
"A customer just had a good experience with [what you did]. Write a short, warm message asking for a review that makes it easy for them (tell them roughly what to mention and where to leave it). No pressure, my voice."
2
Reply to a great review
"A customer left this positive review: [paste it]. Write a warm, genuine reply that thanks them specifically, without sounding copy pasted. 2 sentences."
3
Reply to a critical review
"A customer left this critical review: [paste it]. Write a calm, professional public reply that takes it seriously, does not get defensive, and offers to make it right offline. Short, human, no corporate tone."
4
Turn a happy message into a testimonial
"A customer sent me this kind message: [paste it]. Draft a short testimonial I could use with their permission, keeping their words and meaning intact. Also write me a one line message asking their permission to share it."
5
Build a quick feedback survey
"Write me 5 short questions to ask customers after [service] to find out what is working and what to improve. Mix of quick ratings and one open question. Plain wording, nothing leading."
6
Find the themes in your reviews
"Here are recent reviews and comments: [paste 10 to 20, names removed]. Pull out the themes: what people love, what frustrates them, and the exact phrases they use. Quote, do not paraphrase. Group into a short summary I can act on."
7
Turn a review into a post
"Here is a review I have permission to share: [paste it]. Turn it into a short social post that leads with the customer's own words and adds a line of context. Keep it honest, no exaggeration, my voice."
8
Win back an unhappy customer
"A customer had a poor experience: [what happened]. Write a private message that acknowledges it sincerely, offers a fair way to make it right, and leaves the door open without grovelling. Warm and specific."
9
Ask why someone didn't book
"Someone enquired but did not go ahead. Write a short, low pressure message asking, if they are open to it, what held them back. Make clear I genuinely want to learn, not to sell them again. 2 to 3 sentences."
10
Make a repeatable review-request template
"Build me a review-request template I can send after every [job or service], with [brackets] for the personal detail. Warm, specific, easy to say yes to. Include a shorter follow up version for people who do not reply the first time."

Workflow
Content and social
A week of content started, not a blank page.
1
Plan a month of posts
"Act as my content planner. I am a [what you do] for [who you serve]. Give me 12 post ideas for 4 weeks, each mapped to one job: teach something useful, build trust, or invite people to work with me. Realistic for posting twice a week in my voice."
2
Turn one idea into a week
"Here is one thing I want to say: [paste idea]. Repurpose it into a 6 slide carousel outline, a 30 second video script, and 3 caption hooks. Keep my voice consistent across all three."
3
Write a caption in your voice
"Using this voice: [paste 3 things I have written], write a caption about [topic]. 5 short sentences, second person, one idea, end with a question. No hashtags, no em dashes."
4
Turn a customer question into a post
"Customers keep asking me: [paste question]. Turn my answer into a helpful post that teaches without giving everything away and shows I know my stuff. Include a hook and a soft call to action. My voice."
5
Draft five hooks
"Write me 5 different opening lines for a post about [topic] aimed at [who you serve]. Each should stop the scroll and read well at thumbnail size. No fearmongering, no 'you're falling behind.' Specific and honest."
6
Testimonial into a carousel
"Here is a testimonial I have permission to share: [paste it]. Outline a 6 slide carousel that builds from the problem the customer had to the result, in a way that teaches the reader something too. Keep it truthful, no invented numbers."
7
Short video script
"Write me a 30 to 45 second video script about [topic] for [who you serve]. Hook in the first line, one clear point, one takeaway, and a spoken call to action at the end. Conversational, the way I would actually say it."
8
Batch a week of Stories
"Give me 7 simple Story ideas for this week that keep me visible without much effort: a mix of behind the scenes, a quick tip, a question for my audience, and a soft offer. One line brief for each."
9
Repurpose an email or blog
"Here is something longer I already wrote: [paste it]. Pull out 3 social posts hidden inside it, each with its own angle and hook. Keep my voice, and do not just chop it up, make each stand on its own."
10
Set your content pillars
"Help me define 4 or 5 content pillars for my [business type] so I am not guessing what to post. For each pillar, give a one line description and 3 example post ideas that fit it. Grounded in what my customers actually care about."

Workflow
Bookings
Confirmations and reminders that send themselves, in your voice.
1
Booking confirmation
"Write a booking confirmation message for a customer who just booked [service] for [when]. Confirm the key details, tell them anything they need to do or bring, and set a warm tone. Under 80 words, my voice."
2
A reminder that reduces no-shows
"Write a friendly reminder to send [X hours or days] before an appointment for [service]. Confirm the time, make it easy to reschedule if they truly cannot make it, and gently note why turning up matters. Warm, not naggy."
3
Handle a reschedule or cancellation
"A customer needs to reschedule or cancel [appointment]. Write a reply that makes it easy, restates my policy on [notice or fees] kindly, and offers the next available options. No guilt trip."
4
Pre-appointment prep
"Write a short message to send before [service] telling the customer how to prepare and what to expect, so they turn up ready. Clear, reassuring, bullet points if it helps. My voice."
5
Waitlist update
"Write a message updating someone on my waitlist for [service]. Be honest about where they stand, keep them feeling valued, and tell them the one thing that happens next. Warm, 3 sentences."
6
Turn services into a clear booking menu
"Here are the services I offer: [list them with rough prices and times]. Turn this into a clear, easy to scan booking menu a customer can choose from without confusion. Plain names, one line each on what they get."
7
Explain deposits or payment terms
"I want to introduce [a deposit / payment terms] for bookings because [reason]. Write a short, friendly explanation customers will read at booking that makes the reason clear and feels fair, not corporate. 3 to 4 sentences."
8
Group or class booking
"Write a message for people booking into [a group session or class] on [date]. Cover the essentials (time, place, what to bring), build a little anticipation, and keep it warm and simple. Under 90 words."
9
No-show follow-up
"A customer missed their appointment for [service] without cancelling. Write a message that checks they are okay, avoids blame, and makes it easy to rebook. Kind first, practical second. 3 sentences."
10
What to expect after booking
"Write a 'what happens next' note to send right after someone books [service] for the first time, so they feel looked after. Walk them through the steps in plain language, reassure any nerves, and sound like a person, not a system."

Workflow
Follow-ups
The quote, the invoice, the check-in that usually slips.
1
Thank you after a first purchase
"Write a warm thank you to send after a customer's first [purchase or visit]. Make them feel genuinely appreciated, set up what to expect next, and keep it short. My voice, no upselling in this one."
2
Chase an unpaid invoice, politely
"An invoice for [what it was for] is [X days] overdue. Write a polite, professional reminder that assumes the best, makes it easy to pay, and keeps the relationship warm. Firm but friendly, no passive aggression."
3
Follow up on a quote
"I sent a quote for [work] and have not heard back. Write a follow up that checks it landed, offers to answer any questions, and gently invites a decision without pressure. 3 to 4 sentences."
4
Check in after delivery
"A customer received [product or service] recently. Write a short check in to make sure they are happy, invite any questions, and open the door to the next step if it is natural. Caring, not salesy."
5
Re-engage a quiet customer
"A past customer has not been in touch for a while. They previously came to me for [what]. Write a warm win back message that acknowledges the gap without guilt and offers one easy reason to come back. 4 sentences."
6
Ask for the next booking at the right time
"Based on when they last used [service], it is about time [customer] booked again. Write a helpful nudge that frames rebooking as looking after themselves or their business, with one clear way to book. Warm, low pressure."
7
A "just checking this landed" nudge
"I sent [something] and want to make sure it did not get lost. Write a light, one line friendly nudge that is easy to reply to and does not sound impatient."
8
Seasonal or occasion check-in
"Write a short, genuine check in I can send customers around [season or occasion] that is warm and human, not a thinly disguised sales pitch. It can mention [offer] lightly at the end, or not at all. My voice."
9
Turn a one-off into a repeat
"A customer bought [one-off thing] once and has not returned. Write a message that reminds them of the value they got and suggests a natural next thing, without being pushy. 3 to 4 sentences."
10
Build a simple follow-up sequence
"Design me a simple follow up sequence after a customer's first [purchase or service]: what to send and roughly when, over the following month, to thank them, check in, and invite them back. 4 or 5 steps, one line each on the message and timing. Keep it human, not a funnel."
